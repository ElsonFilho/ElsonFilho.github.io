About

Why I Do This

I have been solving hard problems with data and algorithms for over two decades, starting with neural network-based systems in Brazil, and arriving today, in Switzerland, building multi-agent AI systems that synthesise research and reason across documents. The geography changed. São Paulo, Lisbon, Zurich. The tools changed beyond recognition. But the thing that drives me never did. I need hard problems. Not as ambition. As a condition. Not the version of me performing competence. The real one.

"When a real-world problem is in front of me, everything else goes quiet — defining the right approach, refining data transformations, selecting the most appropriate algorithm, optimising for production — that is when I feel most like myself."

I have spent those years inside financial services, life sciences, retail, and manufacturing, building solutions that mattered to real organisations. Fraud systems that caught what rule engines missed. Predictive models that changed how companies work. AI systems that made research faster. The work was always different. The state it put me in never was.

I am sharing this because I think it matters to be honest about why we do what we do, especially when the easy answer would be to talk about titles and certifications and years of experience. Those things are real, and I am proud of them, but they are not the reason. The reason is simpler and harder to say out loud: I am someone who cannot stop building, cannot stop learning, cannot stop looking for the next problem that is worth my full attention. And I have never wanted to do that alone.

The best moments in my career were not the ones where I figured something out in isolation. They were the ones where a team figured it out together, where someone I was mentoring suddenly saw it, where the collective got somewhere none of us could have reached individually. Craft, for me, has always been a shared practice. And like any craftsman, what I am always looking for is the work that deserves it and the people worth doing it with.

Outside of work, I try to build a life that reflects my values, not just fill the hours between projects. I have lived across several countries and carried different cultures with my family at every step. I ride bicycles wherever the mountains and roads take me, in all seasons, including winter, partly because that terrain demands full presence and partly because I had to fight to get it back. A few years ago I went through an ankle fusion surgery. That experience taught me to respect and value the journey itself, the effort and dedication it takes to overcome chronic pain, endure a hard recovery, a difficult project, a long ride. Not only as a means to an end, but as a way to live better. I carry that into everything now: the capacity to keep going is not about talent or circumstance. It is about enjoying the journey, knowing what you are unwilling to give up.

I play guitar, another practice that demands full presence and rewards only patience and time. These are not escapes from my professional life. They are the same person, in different terrain.
